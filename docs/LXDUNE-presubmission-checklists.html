EDSE357 — Assessment Task 1

EDSE357 AT1 — Pre-Submission Checklist

Progress: 0/0 checked
Part A — Digital Resource Critique
Recommendation if not checked Name the investigation explicitly at the start of Part A — the discipline, the page number, and the year level. Your critique should be clearly anchored to one specific practical, not a general resource about science.
Recommendation if not checked Replace descriptive sentences with evaluative ones. Use a sentence frame: "This resource would/would not support students because it ___, which means students are likely to ___." Description tells the reader what the resource is. Critique tells them what it does — or fails to do — for learning.
Recommendation if not checked Name the pedagogical approach and explain how the resource does or does not support it. Use a sentence frame: "A constructivist approach requires students to ___. This resource ___ that by ___." Avoid simply stating "this is constructivist" without explaining the connection.
Recommendation if not checked Identify the specific Working Scientifically strand the resource addresses (e.g. Questioning and Predicting, Planning Investigations, Processing Data). Then explain whether the resource develops that skill deeply or only superficially. "This resource addresses the Working Scientifically outcome ___ because it requires students to ___. However, it does not require students to ___, which limits its depth."
Recommendation if not checked At least one peer-reviewed source should be integrated into the critique — not just listed. Use a sentence frame: "___ (Author, Year) argues that effective resources for scientific inquiry should ___. This resource ___ that criterion because ___." Aim for literature that justifies your evaluative position, not literature that just describes inquiry generally.
Part B — Risk Assessment, Equipment List & Method
Recommendation if not checked RiskAssess is a revision tool, not a tick-box. Go back through your method step by step and ask: "Does this step reflect what RiskAssess identified as a control or mitigation?" If RiskAssess flagged a hazard and your method does not address it in the procedure itself, revise the step — not just the hazard list. A common gap: students add safety information to the risk assessment section but leave the original method unchanged.
Recommendation if not checked Safety should appear at the point in the procedure where it is relevant, not in a separate section students might skip. Use a sentence frame: "Step 3: [action] — ensure [specific control measure, e.g. safety goggles are worn, fume cupboard is in use] before proceeding." If your safety information only appears in the RiskAssess output and not in the method, a student following your instructions could perform the practical without ever seeing it.
Recommendation if not checked Read your method as if you have never done this practical before. Ask: "Would I know what to do next at every step?" Gaps often appear at setup, measurement, and data recording stages. Add specificity: quantities, equipment settings, timing, and what to do if something goes wrong.
Recommendation if not checked State PPE at the start of the method and at relevant steps. For emergencies, include what a student should do — not just what to avoid. Use a sentence frame: "In the event of [specific incident], students should immediately [specific action]. Do not [specific risk behaviour]."
Part C — Depth Study Design
Recommendation if not checked The aim should name what students will investigate and why it connects to the syllabus. Use a sentence frame: "The aim of this Depth Study is for students to ___, which addresses [outcome code] by requiring them to ___." A vague aim (e.g. "students will learn about ecosystems") does not demonstrate syllabus alignment.
Recommendation if not checked Copy the outcome codes and relevant content dot points from the NSW syllabus PDF. Then explain how the Depth Study addresses each one — do not leave the connection implicit. Syllabus extracts do not count toward word count.
Recommendation if not checked The OEI requires students to drive their own inquiry — but they need enough structure to get started. Check: have you explained what a valid inquiry question looks like for this topic? Have you shown what students need to produce? If your instructions assume students already know how to design an OEI, add scaffolding steps.
Recommendation if not checked List the tangible outputs students submit or present at each stage (e.g. a written plan, a data table, a draft hypothesis, an annotated diagram). Without clear products, a teacher cannot assess engagement or provide feedback at check-in points.
Recommendation if not checked Identify at least two points in the five hours where a teacher would check in. For each, specify: what the student submits or shows, what feedback focuses on, and how feedback connects to the next stage of work. Use a sentence frame: "At the end of Hour 2, students submit their draft inquiry question. Teacher feedback focuses on whether the question is testable and connected to the nominated outcome."
Part D — Reflective Self-Assessment
Recommendation if not checked Shift from describing the task to analysing your learning. Use a sentence frame: "I used to think ___ about [practical investigations / risk assessment / depth studies]. Working through this task changed my thinking because ___." A description of what the task involved is not a reflection on learning.
Recommendation if not checked Name one concrete change you will make in your future practice. Use a sentence frame: "In my future classroom, I will ___ because this task showed me that ___." Avoid vague statements like "I will be more aware of safety" — explain what that awareness will look like in practice.
EDSE357 — Assessment Task 2

EDSE357 AT2 — Pre-Submission Checklist

Progress: 0/0 checked
Professional Judgement & Critical Evaluation (35%)
Recommendation if not checked Replace verdict sentences with reasoned ones. Use a sentence frame: "I would [use / not use / adapt] this resource because it [does / does not] ___. In a Year ___ science classroom, this means students would ___." A professional judgement names the consequence of the decision, not just the decision itself.
Recommendation if not checked Even resources you would use have limitations, and resources you would not use may have value in a different context. Add a balancing sentence to each critique: "While this resource is strong in ___, it is limited by ___, which would need to be addressed by ___."
Recommendation if not checked Ask: Is the science in this resource correct? Is it current? Is the source credible? For digital resources especially, a brief comment on accuracy and authority is expected. Use a sentence frame: "The scientific content of this resource is [accurate / outdated / incomplete] because ___."
Recommendation if not checked Name specific cohort characteristics (language background, prior knowledge, access to equipment, cultural context) and explain how the resource does or does not account for them. Use a sentence frame: "For a cohort that includes ___, this resource [supports / creates a barrier] because ___."
Diversity & Intentional Curation (15%)
Recommendation if not checked Use the checklist in the task instructions to confirm coverage: textbook chapter/section, digital resource, first-hand investigation, second-hand data investigation, external expert, assessment item. At least one resource must be applicable for Life Skills. If any type is missing, add a resource before submitting.
Recommendation if not checked The marker wants to see that your collection is intentional, not just compliant. Add a short rationale to your collection — either in an introduction or woven into individual critiques — that explains how the resources work together for a Year 11–12 cohort in your chosen discipline. Use a sentence frame: "I selected this combination because it gives students exposure to ___, which is important for ___ because ___."
Curriculum Literacy & Assessment Alignment (30%)
Recommendation if not checked Copy outcome codes and content dot points directly from the NSW syllabus PDF for your discipline. Do not paraphrase outcomes — the marker needs to see you can read the syllabus accurately. Then add one sentence explaining the link: "This resource addresses [outcome code] because it requires students to ___." Syllabus extracts do not count toward word count.
Recommendation if not checked Senior science is as much about Working Scientifically skills as content knowledge. For each resource, identify the relevant Working Scientifically strand and explain how the resource develops it. Use a sentence frame: "This resource develops [Working Scientifically strand] by requiring students to ___, at a level of [independence / structure] appropriate for Year ___."
Recommendation if not checked For at least some resources — particularly the assessment item — explain what evidence the resource produces and how a teacher would use it to make an assessment decision. Use a sentence frame: "This resource could be used as [formative / summative] assessment because it produces evidence of ___, which can be evaluated against ___."
Scholarly Grounding & Pedagogical Insight (20%)
Recommendation if not checked Replace floating citations with applied ones. Use a sentence frame: "___ (Author, Year) argues that [pedagogical principle]. This resource [does / does not] reflect that principle because ___." Aim for fewer sources used precisely rather than many listed without purpose.
Recommendation if not checked Name a learning theory (constructivism, inquiry-based learning, cognitive load theory, UDL) and explain how it shapes your evaluation of the resource. Use a sentence frame: "From a constructivist perspective, effective resources should ___. This resource [meets / falls short of] that standard because ___." Theory should strengthen your professional judgement — not appear as a separate section disconnected from your critique.
EDSE358 — Assessment Task 1

EDSE358 AT1 — Pre-Submission Checklist

Progress: 0/0 checked
Part A — Task Outline & Forum Post
Recommendation if not checked Copy the outcome code(s) directly from the NSW syllabus — do not paraphrase. Then add one sentence explaining the link: "This task addresses [outcome code] because it requires students to ___." Vague outcome references (e.g. "relates to Stage 6 science") will not demonstrate the alignment the rubric requires.
Recommendation if not checked Alignment means the model, the outcomes, and what you are assessing all point to the same thing. Ask: "Does the learning model I have chosen naturally lead students toward the outcome? And does my assessment actually capture evidence of that outcome?" If any of the three are pointing in different directions, revise before submitting.
Recommendation if not checked The forum post should tell your peers what you are planning clearly enough that they could give useful feedback. It is not a draft of the full unit — it is a professional summary. Use a sentence frame: "For this unit I am designing a [task type] for Year [level] [discipline]. The assessment targets [outcome] by asking students to ___. The main challenge I am designing around is ___."
Part B — Five-Week Unit
Recommendation if not checked Each week should build on the last. Ask: "If a student did Week 1 only, could they do Week 2 without Week 1?" If yes, your sequence may not have clear progression. Add explicit connections between weeks — for example, a skill introduced in Week 2 should be applied or extended in Week 3.
Recommendation if not checked Constructivism should be visible in how lessons are structured — students building on prior knowledge, active construction of understanding, collaborative sense-making. If constructivism only appears in your justification essay and not in the lesson activities themselves, revise at least two lessons to make the approach explicit. Use a sentence frame: "In this lesson, students first ___ (activate prior knowledge), then ___ (construct new understanding by), and finally ___ (consolidate by)."
Recommendation if not checked Generic differentiation (e.g. "I will provide extra support where needed") does not meet the rubric. Name the cohort characteristics you are designing for — including Aboriginal and/or EAL/D learners — and describe the specific adjustment. Use a sentence frame: "For students who ___, I will ___ in Week ___ so that they can ___ without the barrier of ___."
Recommendation if not checked Resources should deepen conceptual learning — not just fill time. For each resource, ask: "How does this resource move students closer to completing the model task?" If the connection is not clear, either replace the resource or add a sentence explaining how it is used.
Recommendation if not checked Substitutive ICT replaces a non-digital task with a digital one without adding value (e.g. typing up notes instead of writing them). Purposeful ICT changes what is possible (e.g. using a simulation to explore conditions that can't be replicated in a school lab). Revise your ICT to explain what it enables that a non-digital approach could not.
Recommendation if not checked A formative task needs three things: what students produce, what feedback focuses on, and how the feedback connects to the next stage of learning. Use a sentence frame: "At the end of Week ___, students submit ___. Teacher feedback focuses on ___, so that students can improve ___ before ___." If your formative tasks are present but feedback is vague ("teacher provides feedback on progress"), revise to make the feedback actionable.
Part C — Justification Essays
Recommendation if not checked Shift from description to justification. Use a sentence frame: "I chose ___ because ___, which is important for this cohort because ___. This decision is supported by ___ (Author, Year) who argues ___." If your essay reads as a summary of your unit rather than a reasoned argument for your choices, revise to foreground the reasoning.
Recommendation if not checked Every major decision should connect to at least one of: what the syllabus requires, what the cohort needs, or what the literature recommends. If a decision is only justified by "it is good pedagogy generally," strengthen it by naming the specific cohort or outcome it serves.
Recommendation if not checked The selection criteria should be explicit. Use a sentence frame: "I selected this resource over alternatives because it ___. For this cohort, this matters because ___." Avoid simply describing what each resource contains — explain the professional reasoning behind choosing it.
Recommendation if not checked Literature should strengthen your reasoning, not decorate it. Use a sentence frame: "___ (Author, Year) argues ___. My unit reflects this by ___." If your citations only appear in the reference list or as parenthetical additions to sentences that already make their point, revise to show how the literature actually shaped your decisions.
Part D — Reflection
Recommendation if not checked Shift from describing the task to analysing what it revealed about your thinking. Use a sentence frame: "Before this task, I assumed ___. Working through [specific part] changed my understanding of ___ because ___." A reflection that describes what the task involved rather than what you learned from it will not demonstrate the depth the rubric requires.
Recommendation if not checked Analytical reflection explains the mechanism of change, not just the outcome. Use a sentence frame: "My thinking changed because ___ showed me that ___. I now understand ___ differently, which means in my future teaching I will ___." Avoid phrases like "this was a valuable learning experience" without explaining what specifically was learned and why it matters.
Recommendation if not checked Name one specific change you will make. Use a sentence frame: "In my future Stage 6 classroom, I will ___ because this task showed me ___. Without this change, ___." Vague future commitments ("I will be more aware of differentiation") are not sufficient — describe what it will look like in practice.
