LO1 — Planning lesson sequences with constructivist pedagogy (GTSDs 1.2.1, 2.2.1, 2.3.1, 2.4.1, 3.1.1, 3.2.1)

This is the broadest outcome in the unit — it spans how you structure a teaching sequence, how you embed constructivist approaches, how you respond to the given cohort, and how you plan for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives. Modules 1, 3A, and 3B build this progressively.

In Assessment 1, LO1 is assessed primarily through Part B (your unit program) and Part C Essay 1. Your program needs to show a clear sequence where each week builds on the last, with constructivist pedagogy embedded in activities — not just mentioned in the justification. The B:Unit4 criterion now also asks you to articulate a coherent evaluation strategy across the five weeks: how do your formative and summative tasks together tell you whether students have achieved the outcomes?

In Assessment 2, your critique (Part A) and redesign (Part B) both draw on LO1 — you need to show you can read a task for its syllabus alignment and sequence logic, and improve it.

LO2 — Resources, ICT, safety, and diverse learners (GTSDs 1.3.1, 1.4.1, 2.4.1, 2.5.1, 2.6.1, 3.4.1)

This outcome has three distinct dimensions that all need to appear in your unit: purposeful use of ICT, safe approaches to practical work, and responsiveness to the given cohort including Aboriginal students and EAL/D learners. Module 3B is where you develop all three before assessment.

The two new forum activities in Module 3B are your key preparation for Assessment 1. Use them seriously: the ICT activity asks you to explain what each tool makes possible that a non-digital approach could not — that is exactly the thinking required in AT1 Part C Essay 2. The Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander forum asks you to embed a perspective as integral to the science, not as an add-on — that thinking is assessed in AT1 B:Unit1.

In Assessment 1, the B:Unit2 rubric now explicitly assesses safety across all performance bands — a student can no longer score well by ignoring safety. Your resources also need to demonstrate breadth: digital tools, primary sources, practical materials, and more.

In Assessment 2, your redesigned task (Part B) must show specific differentiation for the named cohort, including adjustments that increase access without lowering expectations.

LO3 — Activities, assessment design, and syllabus programming (GTSDs 2.2.1, 2.3.1, 3.1.1, 5.1.1, 5.2.1)

LO3 is the most heavily assessed outcome in the unit — it appears across both assessment tasks and draws on five modules of teaching. The key insight is that selecting activities and designing assessment are the same kind of thinking: both require you to be precise about what students are doing and why it connects to a specific outcome.

The Module 4B forum is your most important formative preparation. Finding a real Stage 6 science assessment task, mapping it to outcomes, and identifying its alignment strengths and gaps is exactly what AT2 Part A asks you to do — at a higher level, with literature support. Do not treat the forum as a minor task.

In Assessment 1, B:Unit3 now requires a range of activity types — a unit built entirely on one mode (e.g., all written tasks) will not score at the HD level. B:Unit4 asks you to show a coherent evaluation strategy across the five weeks, not just individual formative tasks.

In Assessment 2, alignment is assessed as a distinct criterion. It is not enough for your redesigned task to seem reasonable — you need to show that outcomes, instructions, evidence, and criteria all point to the same thing.

LO4 — Planning feedback and reflective practice (GTSDs 5.2.1, 6.1.1, 6.2.1, 6.3.1, 6.4.1)

LO4 has two distinct dimensions that are now assessed separately. The first — planning timely and purposeful feedback — is new to AT1 as a named criterion. The second — reflective practice — has always been assessed but is now explicitly separated so each dimension receives dedicated attention.

The new Module 4D feedback-writing activity is critical preparation for AT1 D1. Before this trimester, students encountered the cognitive demand of feedback planning for the first time in high-stakes assessment. The new activity gives you practice: you write 100–150 words of feedback on an anonymised student response, post it to the forum, and respond to a peer. Use it to develop feedback language that is specific, actionable, and forward-looking.

In Assessment 1 D1, your unit program must include a clearly articulated feedback plan specifying: when feedback will be provided, what form it takes, how it will be differentiated for the given cohort including Aboriginal students and EAL/D learners, and how it connects to the next stage of learning. Generic statements about providing feedback will not meet this criterion.

In Assessment 1 D2, your reflection should be analytical — not a description of what you did, but an account of how your thinking changed and what that means for your future practice. Use a sentence frame if helpful: 'Before this task I assumed ___. Working through ___ changed my understanding because ___.'

In Assessment 2, your redesigned task (Part B) must include a purposeful feedback checkpoint — specify who gives feedback, when, in what form, and how students are expected to act on it.
