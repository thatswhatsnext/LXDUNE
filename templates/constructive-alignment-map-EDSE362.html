LO1 — Science content, structure, and practical integration (GTSD 2.1.1)

This outcome is about knowing your science deeply enough to teach it practically. You develop LO1 across Topics 1, 2, 3, 4, 7, and 8 — pay particular attention to how science content connects to the K–10 continuum and how practical activities can make abstract concepts accessible. Your forum posts in Topics 1–4 are your practice ground — use them to test your thinking before assessment.

In Assessment 1, Part A rewards accurate syllabus mapping and well-chosen practicals. In Assessment 2, Part A (syllabus criterion, 14 marks) and the highlight task (9 marks) both draw directly on this outcome — your knowledge of content structure needs to be precise and applied, not general.

LO2 — Logical, sequential teaching sequences (GTSDs 2.2.1, 2.3.1)

This outcome is about designing teaching that builds — where each lesson makes the next one possible. Topics 1, 6, and 7 develop this thinking. The strand mapping activity in Topic 1 and the programming forum in Topic 6 are your key practice opportunities — treat them seriously, not as box-ticking exercises.

In Assessment 1, sequencing is embedded in the holistic Part A criterion. In Assessment 2, logical sequence is assessed twice — in the syllabus criterion (14 marks) and the teaching sequence criterion (13 marks). A weak sequence in A2 affects 27 marks. Plan your unit so that each lesson has a clear purpose in the overall arc.

LO3 — Plan sequences using student learning, content, and teaching strategies (GTSD 3.2.1)

This outcome asks you to connect what you know about how students learn with what you know about the content and your teaching strategies. Topics 3, 4, and 6 build this capacity — the constructivism activity in Topic 4 is the most important practice opportunity for LO3.

In Assessment 1, Part A's pedagogy cluster and Part B's justification both target this outcome. In Assessment 2, the teaching sequence criterion (13 marks) requires you to articulate your constructivist approach, strategies, and learning goals explicitly. Vague references to "student-centred learning" will not score well — you need to show how specific strategies respond to specific learning needs.

LO4 — Evaluate programs and design assessment with embedded strategies (GTSDs 3.6.1, 5.3.1, 5.3.2)

This outcome is not assessed in Assessment 1 — which makes the Topic 6 critique forum critical. Before you design your own assessment task in A2, you need to practise identifying alignment gaps and rubric flaws in someone else's task first. Use the T6 activity seriously — the quality of your A2 Part C work depends on having done that analytical groundwork.

In Assessment 2, alignment is assessed as a distinct criterion — it is not enough for your task to seem reasonable. You need to show that outcomes, instructions, evidence, and criteria all point to the same thing. Part C carries 18 marks across three criteria: task design (6m), internal alignment (6m), and rubric quality (6m). Each rewards a different dimension of assessment literacy.

LO5 — Responsive strategies for diverse learners (GTSDs 1.3.1, 1.4.1, 2.4.1, 2.5.1)

This outcome has the richest assessment coverage in the unit — it appears in A1 Part A, A1 Part B, A2 Part A, and twice in A2 Part B. That breadth reflects how central inclusive practice is to good science teaching.

The challenge is that your only practice opportunity before A1 is the Topic 5 reflective post — which is reflective, not applied. To prepare well, go beyond reflection in Topic 5: think through a specific cohort, identify real barriers, and draft actual adjustments. When you arrive at Assessment 1 Part A, you need to produce specific, justified adjustments — not general statements about valuing diversity. In Assessment 2, the adjustments criterion (6m), Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander and literacy criterion (3m), and inclusion criterion (3m) all require you to show how your program design responds to real learner needs.

LO6 — Professional learning rationale and relevant sources (GTSDs 5.3.2, 5.4.1, 6.2.1, 6.4.1, 7.4.1)

This outcome appears in both assessments through the reflection tasks — but the prompts have been deliberately redesigned to require specificity. Generic reflections about "learning a lot" or "improving time management" will not meet this outcome.

In Assessment 1 Part C, you need to name a specific professional learning source and justify why it is relevant to your development as a science teacher. In Assessment 2 Part D, you need to provide evidence of program evaluation and name an external professional or community source. The five GTSDs attached to LO6 span professional engagement, collaboration, and continuous improvement — your reflection needs to show you understand these as ongoing professional obligations, not one-off tasks.
