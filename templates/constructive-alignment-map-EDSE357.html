LO1 — Science content knowledge and teaching strategies (GTSD 2.1.1)

This outcome runs through the whole unit — almost every topic develops your knowledge of science content and how to teach it. The important shift as you move from content knowledge to this outcome is from knowing the science to knowing how to make it accessible and meaningful for senior secondary students.

In Assessment 1, Part A asks you to critique a digital resource for a specific practical investigation — your critique must link to syllabus elements and Working Scientifically outcomes, which requires you to understand the content and teaching purpose deeply. Part C requires you to design a Depth Study that is accurate to the syllabus and realistic for five hours of class work — that accuracy is LO1 in action.

In Assessment 2, the Curriculum Literacy criterion (30%) directly assesses whether your resource selections are precisely aligned to syllabus outcomes and content. Vague or incorrect syllabus references will cost marks. Use the actual NSW syllabus document when completing your resource table — not summaries or textbook chapter headings.

LO2 — Assessment strategies and reporting student progress (GTSDs 5.1.1, 5.4.1)

This outcome is most directly assessed through AT1 Part C3 and the assessment purpose column in the AT2 resource table. Both ask you to think about assessment not as a product students submit, but as information a teacher collects, interprets, and acts on.

In Assessment 1, Part C3 asks you to design check-in points and feedback opportunities within your Depth Study. These are not just milestones — they are formative assessment moments where you, as a teacher, are gathering evidence of student thinking and responding to it. The HD descriptor requires ample feedback opportunities supported by scholarly literature. Think about what you want to know about student progress at each point, and how you will find out.

In Assessment 2, for each resource you must identify its assessment purpose — formative, summative, or diagnostic. This is not a box to tick. Resources that can be used formatively (to gather feedback and adjust teaching) are generally more valuable than resources that only produce a summative grade. Make this judgement explicit in your critique.

LO3 — Investigative skills, cognitive engagement, and safe practice (GTSDs 3.3.1, 4.1.1, 4.2.1, 4.4.1)

LO3 is the most heavily assessed outcome in AT1 — it appears across Parts A, B, and C. The common thread is your ability to design learning experiences that develop students' capacity to investigate, think scientifically, and work safely.

The new Topic 3 forum activity is your most important formative preparation for AT1 Part C2. Use it to develop your thinking about what makes investigation instructions work before you write your own. The key question is: could a student follow these instructions and make genuine scientific progress without asking you what to do next?

In Assessment 1, Part B is the most direct assessment of safe practice — B1 tests the accuracy and completeness of your risk assessment, B2 tests whether safety is integrated into the method rather than treated as an afterthought. Safety is not an add-on — it is a dimension of good science pedagogy.

In Assessment 2, the Curriculum Literacy criterion specifically assesses investigative skills alignment. When you select resources, identify which Working Scientifically outcomes they support and explain why — this demonstrates LO3 at assessment level.

LO4 — ICT strategies and resource evaluation (GTSD 2.6.1)

This outcome asks you to go beyond describing what a digital resource contains — you need to evaluate what it makes possible for student learning. The key evaluative question is: what can students do with this resource that they could not do without it?

The new Topic 5 forum activity is your rehearsal for this thinking before AT2. Use it to develop your evaluative language. Phrases like "this resource allows students to..." and "unlike a static diagram, this simulation enables students to..." demonstrate the kind of thinking LO4 and the AT2 Professional Judgement criterion reward.

In Assessment 1, Part A requires you to critique a digital resource using pedagogical principles of scientific inquiry. Your critique should identify specifically which Working Scientifically outcomes the resource addresses and why it does so effectively or ineffectively — not just describe what the resource shows.

In Assessment 2, at least one of your eight resources must be a digital resource. Your critique of it should demonstrate the same evaluative depth — assess its accuracy, accessibility, pedagogical approach, and what it uniquely enables for senior science students.

LO5 — Professional resources and networks (GTSDs 3.4.1, 7.4.1)

LO5 is not assessed in AT1 — it is assessed entirely through AT2. Your external expert or community representative resource is your primary opportunity to demonstrate this outcome. Choose it deliberately, not as a compliance checkbox.

The Diversity criterion in AT2 now explicitly requires evidence of awareness of professional learning networks and community expertise. Think about what an external expert brings that no textbook or digital resource can: a professional perspective, community knowledge, or discipline expertise that connects the science to the real world beyond the classroom.

When you write your critique of the external expert resource, go beyond describing who they are. Evaluate: what does this expert make possible for senior science students that other resources cannot? How does engaging with community expertise connect to your responsibilities as a teacher to build professional networks and stay current in your discipline?

Topic 8 develops the broader thinking behind LO5 — consider how professional resources and networks can help you be more responsive to diverse learner needs, including Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students and EAL/D learners.

LO6 — Critical textbook analysis (GTSDs 3.4.1, 3.6.1)

LO6 is assessed entirely through AT2 — and your textbook resource critique is where you demonstrate it. This outcome asks you to go well beyond summarising what a textbook chapter covers. You need to evaluate it as a professional: is the science accurate and current? Does it align to the syllabus as written, or does it miss, misrepresent, or extend beyond outcomes? What pedagogical approach does it take, and is that approach suited to the cognitive demands of senior science?

The new Topic 7 forum activity gives you formative practice for exactly this kind of thinking before AT2. Use it seriously — develop your evaluative language and identify what a rigorous textbook critique looks like before the high-stakes submission.

When you select your textbook resource for AT2, choose one that gives you something substantial to evaluate. A textbook chapter that is accurate, current, and well-aligned to the syllabus is worth selecting — and worth saying so, with justification. A chapter with gaps or misalignments is also worth selecting, because analysing those gaps demonstrates exactly the professional judgement LO6 requires.

The Scholarly Grounding criterion (20%) rewards you for using literature to support your evaluative reasoning — cite learning theory or science education research to explain why the textbook's pedagogical approach works or doesn't work for senior secondary learners.
