Part A — Task Outline & Forum Post
Recommendation if not checked Copy the outcome code(s) directly from the NSW syllabus — do not paraphrase. Then add one sentence explaining the link: "This task addresses [outcome code] because it requires students to ___." Vague outcome references (e.g. "relates to Stage 6 science") will not demonstrate the alignment the rubric requires.
Recommendation if not checked Alignment means the model, the outcomes, and what you are assessing all point to the same thing. Ask: "Does the learning model I have chosen naturally lead students toward the outcome? And does my assessment actually capture evidence of that outcome?" If any of the three are pointing in different directions, revise before submitting.
Recommendation if not checked The forum post should tell your peers what you are planning clearly enough that they could give useful feedback. Use a sentence frame: "For this unit I am designing a [task type] for Year [level] [discipline]. The assessment targets [outcome] by asking students to ___. The main challenge I am designing around is ___."
Part B — Five-Week Unit
Recommendation if not checked Each week should build on the last. Ask: "If a student did Week 1 only, could they do Week 2 without Week 1?" If yes, your sequence may not have clear progression. Add explicit connections between weeks — a skill introduced in Week 2 should be applied or extended in Week 3.
Recommendation if not checked Constructivism should be visible in how lessons are structured — students building on prior knowledge, active construction of understanding, collaborative sense-making. If constructivism only appears in your justification essay and not in the lesson activities themselves, revise at least two lessons to make the approach explicit.
Recommendation if not checked Generic differentiation (e.g. "I will provide extra support where needed") does not meet the rubric. Name the cohort characteristics you are designing for — including Aboriginal and/or EAL/D learners — and describe the specific adjustment. Use a sentence frame: "For students who ___, I will ___ in Week ___ so that they can ___ without the barrier of ___."
Recommendation if not checked Resources should deepen conceptual learning — not just fill time. For each resource, ask: "How does this resource move students closer to completing the model task?" If the connection is not clear, either replace the resource or add a sentence explaining how it is used.
Recommendation if not checked Substitutive ICT replaces a non-digital task with a digital one without adding value (e.g. typing up notes instead of writing them). Purposeful ICT changes what is possible (e.g. using a simulation to explore conditions that can't be replicated in a school lab). Revise your ICT to explain what it enables that a non-digital approach could not.
Recommendation if not checked A formative task needs three things: what students produce, what feedback focuses on, and how the feedback connects to the next stage of learning. Use a sentence frame: "At the end of Week ___, students submit ___. Teacher feedback focuses on ___, so that students can improve ___ before ___."
Part C — Justification Essays
Recommendation if not checked Shift from description to justification. Use a sentence frame: "I chose ___ because ___, which is important for this cohort because ___. This decision is supported by ___ (Author, Year) who argues ___." If your essay reads as a summary of your unit rather than a reasoned argument for your choices, revise to foreground the reasoning.
Recommendation if not checked Every major decision should connect to at least one of: what the syllabus requires, what the cohort needs, or what the literature recommends. If a decision is only justified by "it is good pedagogy generally," strengthen it by naming the specific cohort or outcome it serves.
Recommendation if not checked The selection criteria should be explicit. Use a sentence frame: "I selected this resource over alternatives because it ___. For this cohort, this matters because ___." Avoid simply describing what each resource contains — explain the professional reasoning behind choosing it.
Recommendation if not checked Literature should strengthen your reasoning, not decorate it. Use a sentence frame: "___ (Author, Year) argues ___. My unit reflects this by ___." If your citations only appear in the reference list or as parenthetical additions to sentences that already make their point, revise to show how the literature actually shaped your decisions.
Part D1 — Planning timely and purposeful feedback (5 marks)
Recommendation if not checked Be explicit about timing — not just "after submission" but when within the unit sequence. For example: "Formative feedback on draft outlines will be provided within five days of submission in week 4, allowing students time to revise before the final task deadline."
Recommendation if not checked Name the mode(s) explicitly and explain why that mode suits this task and cohort. Avoid vague terms like "feedback will be given" — describe the format students will actually receive.
Recommendation if not checked This is a required element, not optional. Address at minimum: how feedback will be accessible to EAL/D learners (e.g., visual supports, plain language) and how feedback will be culturally responsive for Aboriginal students. A sentence frame: "For EAL/D learners, feedback will be ___. For Aboriginal students, I will ___ to ensure feedback is ___."
Recommendation if not checked Show the feedback loop — not just what feedback says, but how it changes what you do next. Use a sentence frame: "If student responses show ___, I will adjust ___ in the following week by ___."
Recommendation if not checked Name the specific outcomes the feedback addresses. For example: "Feedback on Part B targets outcome [X] — students should be able to ___ — by helping them see where their resource selection does or does not demonstrate ___."
Part D2 — Reflective self-assessment (5 marks)
Recommendation if not checked Your reflection should be analytical, not descriptive. Do not summarise what the task asked you to do. Instead, identify one specific thing that changed in your thinking and explain why it changed. Use a sentence frame: "Before this task, I assumed ___. Working through ___ changed my understanding because ___."
Recommendation if not checked Name one specific change you will make. Vague future commitments ("I will be more aware of differentiation") are not sufficient — describe what it will look like in practice. Use a sentence frame: "In my future Stage 6 classroom, I will ___ because this task showed me ___."
